# Introduction

Colorectal cancer (CRC) remains one of the most prevalent malignancies worldwide, representing a significant global health burden with over one million new cases diagnosed annually and contributing substantially to cancer-related mortality rates [1]. Early detection and intervention have been demonstrated to dramatically improve patient prognosis and survival outcomes, positioning CRC as a disease where timely clinical action can yield substantial therapeutic benefits. In this context, colorectal polyps—particularly adenomatous polyps—serve as critical precursor lesions that, if identified and removed during their pre-malignant stages, can substantially reduce the risk of progression to invasive malignancy. The identification of these lesions through endoscopic screening represents a cornerstone of modern preventive gastroenterology, yet the clinical efficacy of this approach is fundamentally constrained by the limitations inherent to manual visual assessment.

Conventional colonoscopy, despite its established clinical utility, is burdened by significant diagnostic limitations that compromise its effectiveness as a screening modality. Polyp miss rates remain persistently high, with clinical studies documenting that endoscopists fail to detect between 20% and 30% of adenomatous polyps during routine examinations [2]. These diagnostic failures are attributed to multiple interconnected factors, including operator variability, fatigue-related performance degradation, and the inherent difficulty in visually discriminating diminutive, sessile, or laterally spreading lesions from surrounding mucosa. Furthermore, the subjective nature of endoscopic assessment introduces substantial inter-observer and intra-observer variability in both polyp identification and morphological characterization, creating inconsistencies in clinical decision-making regarding intervention strategies. The challenge is particularly acute in detecting small or flat polyps that exhibit subtle chromatic variations or low-contrast profiles, as these lesions frequently evade visual detection despite their clinical significance as potential precursors to malignancy.

In recent decades, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and deep learning methodologies have emerged as transformative technologies in medical image analysis, demonstrating unprecedented capabilities in detecting and characterizing pathological features across diverse anatomical sites. The application of deep learning to gastrointestinal endoscopy has yielded promising results, with various network architectures—including ResNet, U-Net, and custom-designed models—showing enhanced sensitivity and specificity compared to unaided human assessment [3]. These advances have prompted substantial research investment in developing automated detection systems capable of augmenting endoscopic performance. However, current state-of-the-art models suffer from critical limitations that restrict their clinical translation and real-world utility. Generalization remains problematic, as models trained on specific datasets frequently exhibit substantial performance degradation when applied to patient populations with varying demographics, endoscopic equipment, or imaging protocols. Additionally, many existing architectures function as "black-box" systems that provide classification outputs without mechanistic interpretability, raising concerns regarding clinical adoption in regulated healthcare environments where explainability and trustworthiness are paramount regulatory and ethical requirements.

The integration of segmentation and classification tasks has historically been approached through separate, sequential pipelines, a paradigm that fails to exploit the complementary information inherent to these interconnected tasks. Contemporary literature reveals limited exploration of hybrid architectures that seamlessly integrate advanced segmentation modules with downstream classification components, particularly those incorporating ensemble learning methodologies [4]. Ensemble Extreme Learning Machines (ELM), despite their demonstrated efficacy in diverse machine learning contexts, remain underutilized in the medical imaging domain for polyp classification tasks. Moreover, the clinical translation of deep learning systems remains severely hampered by the "interpretability gap"—the persistent challenge of providing clinically meaningful explanations for algorithmic predictions in a manner that facilitates physician understanding and enables identification of potential failure modes. Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) techniques, while emerging as a research domain, have not been comprehensively integrated with hybrid deep learning-ELM architectures in the context of gastrointestinal polyp detection.

Addressing these multifaceted limitations, this research introduces TR-SE-NET, a sophisticated architecture designed specifically for polyp segmentation through synergistic integration of transformer mechanisms, residual learning pathways, and squeeze-excitation modules. The transformer component captures long-range spatial dependencies and contextual information through self-attention mechanisms, while residual connections facilitate stable gradient propagation and enable training of deeper architectures. Squeeze-excitation blocks provide channel-wise feature recalibration, enhancing the model's capacity to emphasize diagnostically relevant features while suppressing background noise. Beyond segmentation, this work proposes an ensemble Extreme Learning Machine framework for robust polyp classification, leveraging the complementary strengths of multiple ELM models to achieve superior generalization and resilience to data variability. Critically, this investigation incorporates comprehensive explainability mechanisms through XAI techniques, rendering the model's decision-making processes transparent and clinically interpretable. The proposed framework facilitates identification of critical image regions that drive classification decisions, enabling gastroenterologists to validate algorithmic predictions against clinical expertise and fostering the clinical confidence necessary for practical adoption.

The proposed methodology bears direct clinical relevance through its potential to substantially augment colonoscopic efficacy by reducing polyp miss rates and enhancing diagnostic consistency across diverse clinical environments. By systematizing the detection process through intelligent automated systems, the framework addresses variability inherent to manual assessment while maintaining explainability that ensures clinician oversight and intervention. Enhanced accuracy in polyp identification translates directly to improved patient outcomes through earlier intervention in the natural history of colorectal malignancy. Furthermore, the ensemble approach and comprehensive explainability mechanisms establish a foundation for trustworthy clinical deployment, addressing regulatory and ethical concerns that have historically impeded AI adoption in clinical gastroenterology. The real-world applicability of the system is enhanced by its capacity to function across diverse endoscopic equipment and imaging protocols, attributes critical for deployment in heterogeneous clinical settings.
